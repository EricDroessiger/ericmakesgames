Description

Rail Recon was the final project in my 1st Year of University.

The project was so successful it received an award for being the best overall game within year 1 of our University.

After another 6 months of bug fixing and polishing we released the game on Steam on the 23rd of January 2018.

The project brief's goal was to choose a 1980’s reference game and keep the Core Dynamics and Game Feel while reworking the Mechanics and Aesthetics (based on the MDA Model). We also had to get greenlit two times by the teachers to be allowed continuing the project.

Game Inspiration

Rail Recon is inspired by „Elevator Action“ developed by Taito in 1983. The project brief's goal was to choose a game from the 1980’s and re-design its mechanics and aesthetics while keeping the dynamics of the reference game.

I wanted to make sure to follow the project brief and not just modernize the game but make a new game that feels true to the reference game. One of Elevator Action’s core dynamic is Pathfinding. The player has to re-strategize constantly and find a new path, due to the changing environment and partially unpredictable enemy behavior.

To keep the core dynamic of Pathfinding, we had to find a way to limit the player’s movement while also giving enough interesting options. As a result, we created the rail-based movement and built the whole game around it. We created a game in which the player must respond quickly and keep an eye out for a path while being under fire.

Challenges

Deadlines

The biggest challenge during this project (and other teams) were the deadlines. We had two deadlines and on each 50% of the projects got cut and reassigned to other teams.

For the first deadline (Greenlight Pitch) we had two weeks and pitch the concept with a video of a prototype & and art mockup. Challenging for this Deadline was the lack of programming power as our only programmer got ill before the actual deadline. However, we got together as a team and decided to make a video of a paper prototype as an emergency solution. Gladly we were able to use the time left to create an effective pitch and Art Mockups which got the project greenlit.

For the second deadline (First Deliverable), we had another two weeks to create a playable prototype and got an additional 3 members. This was a challenge since we didn’t have much running in-engine.
But thanks to a clear vision and the additional manpower we created a prototype that demonstrated solid gameplay and got approved and complimented by the teachers.

Tools

Due to our unique movement system we didn’t have many pre-built tools within UE4 to place rails and nodes for them. The first level we created (now Level 6) took multiple days of work to get into a presentable state.
Communicating this problem to our programmers proved to be crucial. During the following weeks, they created tools that improved the process and saved 2/3 of time. This gave us Designers more time to iterate.

Lessons Learned

What to keep doing?

  • During the project, I learned how important it is be to listen to your teacher (or other parties) and find the important piece of advice they want to give. If the other party feels listened to they feel more involved in the project and give better feedback as well.
  • I learned, when suggestionsgiven by clients are good but could prove problematic, it's always best to check with the team. If there is a valid reason we shouldn't apply it 1:1.
  • Making Backup Builds as internal deadlines the day before can help reducing stress on the actual deadline as the team can focus more on improving the build.
  • Using Team meetings after deadlines to get a new focus and new motivation within the team.
  • Even if a very challenging situation occurs it is important to focus on the things you can still change and make them even better.

What to do better?

  • Important tasks should be assigned to easy to reach team members for faster communication and iteration.
  • Create Feature teams according to one feature for one sprint and having one main communicator in each team to streamline communication.
  • Improve Leadership skills by finding my own sweet spot between being myself and being strict, when I think it is beneficial for the project or the teamwork.
  • I need to communicate and ask progress updates regularly if the person is not proactive themselves.
  • Close to critical deadlines, I need to be stricter about what goes into the build.
